# Lecture 1: Introduction to CPM II

## Course Philosophy

CPM II continues from CPM I, moving from general model classes to **concrete models for concrete phenomena**. The course adopts a psychological perspective on cognitive systems.

## Two Paradigms of Cognitive Science

### 1. Information Processing (Component-Dominant)

- Cognition as **staged computation**: sensation → perception → attention → memory → decision → response
- Assumes clearly defined stimuli, functions, and components
- Systems are **closed** (or isolated)
- Dynamics tend toward stable attractors (start-stop cognition)
- Models: formal languages, logic, neural networks as function approximators, Bayesian inference

**Modeling aim:** Function realization in response to stimuli
- Expert systems (features + rules)
- Neural networks (stochastic rules)
- Bayesian systems
- Deep learning (feature detection)

### 2. Interaction-Dominant (Ecological/Embodied/Enactive)

- Cognition as **adaptive coordination** with environment
- No easily distinguished stages — continuous perception-action cycles
- Organism is tuned to and coupled with the environment
- Open, self-complexifying systems; equifinal
- Movement "at the beginning" — action is primary

**Modeling aim:** Identification of environmental structures; dynamical description of complex systems
- Discovering stabilities
- Time series analyses & simulations
- Differential equations

### Key Approaches within Interaction-Dominant

| Approach | Core Idea |
|----------|-----------|
| Ecological Psychology (Gibson) | Affordances, direct perception, world as its own model |
| Enactivism (Varela, Thompson) | Cognition through organism-environment dynamic interplay |
| Interactivism (Bickhard) | Cognition as ongoing interaction, not representation |
| Discursive Psychology | Language use in social context |

## The "Figure-Ground Reversal"

The paper "Beyond Single-Mindedness" (Dignemanse et al., 2023) proposes putting **interaction** center-stage:
- Multiple "marginal" approaches (ecological psychology, enactivism, interactional linguistics, social neuroscience) all revolve around a common core: **interaction**
- Studying cognition requires studying **coordination dynamics**

## Examples Across the Course

- **Attention:** Not just bottleneck — necessity for coherent action
- **Memory:** Not just storage — history/experience of the system; is structured environment part of memory?
- **Motor control:** Not "training effectors" — how better control affects perception
- **Coordination with others:** Feeling of agency, emotions, empathy

## Course Motto

> "Seek dynamical in computational and computational in dynamical"

> "Percepts and concepts interpenetrate... Neither, taken alone, knows reality in its completeness." — William James, 1911

## Key Warning
