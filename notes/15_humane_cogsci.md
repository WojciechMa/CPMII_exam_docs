# Lecture 15: Towards Humane Cognitive Science

## Two Senses of "Social Cognition"

### Sense 1: Cognition OF Social Objects
- How we perceive, represent, reason about other people
- Theory of Mind, face recognition, emotion recognition
- Other people as "stimuli" to be processed
- Traditional cognitive science approach

### Sense 2: Cognition IS Social
- Cognition itself is fundamentally shaped by social interaction
- "Action first" in the social world (Vygotsky)
- Cognitive development = product of social collaboration, not just individual processing
- Social interaction is not just a context but constitutive of cognition

> "Human behavior is a developmental product of a wider system than the system of human individual functions." — Vygotsky, 1930

## William James on Social Sensitivity (1884)

> "The most important part of my environment is my fellow-man. The consciousness of his attitude towards me is the perception that normally unlocks most of my shames and indignations and fears."

> "No one can walk across the platform at a public meeting with just the same muscular innervation he uses to walk across his room at home."

Key point: Our bodies are immediately and involuntarily affected by others' attention. This is not "social cognition" as computation — it's embodied, felt, pre-reflective.

## Engaged Epistemology (De Jaegher, 2019)

### The Problem
- Most cognitive science relies on "disengaged" 3rd-person relations
- Scientific = objective, generalizable, detached
- But: most of our knowledge about humans IS **2nd-person knowledge** (gained through direct engagement)

### The Proposal

> "To bring back into epistemology our felt understanding that what matters matters"

**Engaged epistemology** = recognizing that:
- 1st-person experience and 2nd-person engagement CAN be scientific observation
- Translating 1st/2nd person → 3rd person = double work (or destroying what matters)
- We use felt knowledge anyway — make it explicit and rigorous

### "Human knowing is a living, loving knowing"

Based on Kym Maclaren (2002) — relation based on "letting be":
- To engage one has to **let the other be** (not overdetermining or underdetermining)
- **Co-determining:** mutual influence without domination
- **Mutually transforming:** both knower and known are changed
- **Being open:** receptivity to what the other brings

> "Other's actions always situate and determine us. And we, on the other hand, are always situating and determining others through our own actions" — Maclaren, 2002

## Example: Dementia Care

### Standard Approach
- Assess person's skills: e.g., impaired emotion recognition (from photographs)
- Operationalize: variables, measurements, computations

### Engaged Approach
- The last thing to go: **emotional connection** (not measurable skills)
- "What we should know about a person in dementia" ≠ what can be measured
- How to operationalize a "person"? (Bickhard) — It's not measuring operations that define persons
- Models should address processes and organizations that **actually constitute** persons
- Relying on own experience in interaction = data (!)

### Implications for Cognitive Science
- "Cognitive Science as a Science of an Engaged Mind"
- I-we tension: being together AND letting be
- Reflective responding: awareness of social "formatting" by bodily actions and rhythms
- The struggle of being implicated by multiple people/relations

## Summary: What Makes Human Cognition of Humans Special?

According to engaged epistemology:
1. Most knowledge about humans = 2nd-person (not detached observation)
2. This knowing is mutually transforming (not one-directional)
3. Requires "letting be" — genuine openness, not control
4. Traditional 3rd-person science may destroy what it studies
5. Bodies are immediately affected by social presence (James)
6. Felt understanding is epistemically valid, not just "subjective noise"
7. Studying cognition requires studying coordination and interaction
