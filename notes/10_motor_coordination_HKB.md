# Lecture 10: Dynamical Systems — Models of Motor Coordination

## The Primacy of Action

> "We come into the world moving – kicking, flailing, crying, waving; we are precisely not stillborn" — Sheets-Johnston, 2018

### Ecological Psychology

- Environment is present as source of continuous variables
- "World as its own model" (Rodney Brooks, 1990)
- Nature "saves genetic expenditures whenever she can by relying on high probability world events" (Bates, 1979)
- Basic questions:
  - What are the important world events? What is their structure?
  - How is the organism **tuned to and coupled with** world events? (not "how it reacts to stimuli")

### Movement Reveals Knowledge
- Knowledge observable but not easily describable
- Individual and interactive (values realized in action)
- People as "tender measuring devices" — we read meaning in others' movements
- Neglected in science but not by everyday interactants (De Jaegher)

## Interaction-Dominant Systems

> "Behavioral order does not arise from the dynamics of the system components per se, but from the **interactions between components**" — van Orden

Order (functional behavior) is **sculpted** from interactions under proper constraints/control.

## The Degrees of Freedom Problem (Bernstein, 1967)

**Problem:** Every movement can be performed in many ways → requires control over many degrees of freedom in a flexible way.

**"Repetition without repetition"** — no two movements are exactly alike, yet the functional outcome is achieved consistently.

### Solution: Synergies

**Synergy:** Functional and temporary binding of degrees of freedom into a coherent behavior.

**Features of synergies:**
1. **Reduction of system's dimensionality** — fewer things to control
2. **Reciprocal compensation** — dealing with perturbations by adjusting other components

### Example: Speech Production (Kelso et al., 1984)

- Jaw perturbation during production of /baeb/ → **lips** compensate (20-30ms!)
- Jaw perturbation during production of /baez/ → **tongue** compensates (20-30ms!)
- Compensation is **specific to the phase** of movement (opening vs. closing)
- No speech distortion despite perturbation!
- Demonstrates: system organized at level of GOAL, not individual effectors

## Haken-Kelso-Bunz (HKB) Model

### What It Models
Bimanual coordination — rhythmic movement of two limbs (e.g., index fingers).

### Order Parameter
**Relative phase (φ):** the phase difference between two oscillating limbs
- φ = 0° → in-phase (symmetric, both move same direction simultaneously)
- φ = 180° → anti-phase (fingers alternate)

### Control Parameter
**Movement frequency** — experimenter increases speed

### The Potential Function
V(φ) = −a·cos(φ) − b·cos(2φ)

- At low frequency: two stable states (in-phase and anti-phase)
- As frequency increases: ratio b/a changes → anti-phase becomes unstable

### Key Predictions

**Prediction 1: Differential stability**
- In-phase is MORE stable than anti-phase at all frequencies
- Anti-phase shows larger variability (standard deviation of φ)

**Prediction 2: Phase transition (bifurcation)**
- When frequency exceeds critical value → anti-phase **spontaneously switches** to in-phase
- This is a **non-equilibrium phase transition**
- Transition shows:
  - **Critical slowing down:** relaxation time increases near transition (Schöner, Haken & Kelso, 1986)
  - **Critical fluctuations:** variability increases before switch (Kelso et al., 1986)
  - **Hysteresis:** transition point differs depending on direction of frequency change

### Why HKB Matters
- Shows behavioral patterns emerge from dynamics (not programmed)
- Explains when and why coordination breaks down
- Generalizes beyond fingers: walking, speech, social coordination
- Paradigmatic example of self-organization in behavior

## Coordination Dynamics

Broader framework (Kelso):
- Studies transitions between modes of coordination
- Applies to: intrapersonal (within person), interpersonal (between persons)
- Foundation for understanding social behavior (Tognoli et al., 2020)
